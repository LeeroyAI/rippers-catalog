How to Measure Yourself
1

Barefoot Height

Stand against a flat wall with no shoes. Place a book flat on your head against the wall, mark the height, then measure from the floor to the mark. This is your barefoot height — use this number.

2

Inseam Length

Stand with feet shoulder-width apart (no shoes). Place a hardback book between your legs spine-up, pressed firmly against your crotch. Measure from the spine to the floor. This is your inseam — critical for standover clearance.

3

Arm Reach

Stretch both arms out horizontally. Have someone measure fingertip to fingertip. If your wingspan is significantly longer than your height, you may prefer a longer reach — consider sizing up.

4

Test Ride if Possible

Charts are a starting point. Riding position preference, flexibility, and injury history all affect the right fit. If you're between sizes, always test ride both if your local dealer allows it.

Key Geometry Terms Explained
Reach

How long the bike feels

Horizontal distance from the bottom bracket to the top of the head tube. Longer reach = more stretched out position. The single most important number for comparing bikes across brands — more reliable than frame size labels.

Stack

How tall the front end is

Vertical distance from the bottom bracket to the top of the head tube. Higher stack = more upright position. Relevant if you want a taller cockpit without lots of spacers, or if you have back issues.

Head Tube Angle (HTA)

Steering speed & stability

Slacker angle (63–65°) = more stable at speed, better for descending. Steeper angle (67–69°) = quicker, more responsive steering for XC and climbing. Modern enduro bikes run 63–65°; trail bikes 65–67°.

Seat Tube Angle (STA)

Pedalling efficiency

Steeper STA (76–80°) puts your weight directly over the pedals — better power transfer on climbs. Slacker STA (72–75°) pushes you back, which can feel more natural but is less efficient climbing steep terrain.

Chainstay Length

Rear wheel feel

Distance from the bottom bracket to the rear axle. Shorter chainstays (420–430mm) = more playful, easier to lift the front. Longer (440–460mm) = more stability and traction climbing. Affects the whole character of the ride.

Bottom Bracket Height

Cornering clearance

How high the pedals sit above the ground. Lower BB (335–345mm) = better cornering feel and stability. Higher BB (355–375mm) = more pedal clearance on technical terrain. Enduro bikes trend lower; XC bikes trend higher.

Brand Sizing Notes

Frame size labels (S, M, L) are not standardised across brands. A Medium at one brand can feel like a Large at another. Always check the actual reach number in the geometry chart.

Brand Sizing Tendency Notes
Specialized Runs large S often fits riders who'd normally take M elsewhere. Check reach carefully.
Trek True to size Consistent sizing. Their "Fit Finder" tool on the AU site is reliable.
Santa Cruz Runs large Known for long reach. Riders often size down one. Check reach vs height.
Norco True to size Geometry chart is detailed and reliable. Their "Ride Aligned" system adjusts BB and head angle via flip chip.
Giant Varies by model XC models run true; trail/enduro models (Reign, Trance) run slightly large in reach.
Canyon Runs large Direct-to-consumer — no test rides. Conservative sizing down is advised. Their geometry is progressive (long, slack).
YT Industries Runs large Direct-to-consumer. Very progressive geometry — often a size smaller than expected feels right.
Transition True to size Geometry charts are detailed. Longer reach than some brands at the same label.
AMFLOW True to size Starts at M (no S or XS). Minimum recommended height ~165 cm. PX/PR geometry has 40 adjustment positions.
Marin Runs small Compact geometry — many riders size up one from their usual size. Verify reach numbers.

Always verify with the brand's current geometry chart — sizing tendencies can change model year to model year.
